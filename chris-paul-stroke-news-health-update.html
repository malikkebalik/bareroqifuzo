When there is inadequate blood flow to the brain, it can cause a stroke, which kills brain cells. Ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes allude to bleeding or a lack of blood flow. Both put a stop to specific cerebral processes.

One or more of the following may be an indication or symptom of a stroke: difficulty speaking or understanding, dizziness, or loss of vision on one side of the body.

Symptoms and signs of a stroke frequently emerge rapidly. A stroke with symptoms lasting less than one or two hours is known as a transient ischemia attack (TIA), also called a mini-stroke.

Another sign of a hemorrhagic stroke is a severe headache. A stroke may have long-lasting consequences. Long-term complications include pneumonia and the inability to regulate one’s bladder.

Due to their prevalence, preventing strokes has become a top public health priority. Secondary prevention is more efficient than primary protection (as judged by the number needed to treat to prevent one stroke per year).

The statistics supporting primary stroke prevention are described in recent recommendations. Aspirin appears to have no benefits in otherwise healthy people, so it is not advised to consume it.

It offers some defense against a first stroke when a person has a history of myocardial infarction or is more susceptible to cardiovascular illness.

Medication like aspirin, clopidogrel, and dipyridamole may provide respite for people who have already experienced a stroke.
